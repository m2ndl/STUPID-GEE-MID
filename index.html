Discourse Analysis Study Guide

Pragmatics: The study of how context shapes the interpretation of utterances. [cite_start][cite: 174]

  • Example: The sentence "I died" means something different when said by a video game player versus a religious convert. [cite_start][cite: 175]

Discourse Analysis (Traditional): The study of language structure beyond the sentence, focusing on how sentences are organized into coherent texts (like stories, reports, or arguments). [cite_start][cite: 176]

Discourse Analysis (Author's Definition): A merged definition that combines both of the above. [cite_start][cite: 177]

  • It is "the study of the use and impact of oral or written language in conveying and shaping ideas and social realities". [cite_start][cite: 178]

Discourse (Foucault's Definition): A much broader, non-linguistic concept. It refers to a complete system of knowledge, social practices, institutions, and power relations that shapes social reality. [cite_start][cite: 179]

  • Example: The "discourse of madness" describes how society (through institutions, laws, and medicine) has treated madness differently over time, framing it as wisdom, then illness, and now (for some) as "neurodiversity". [cite_start][cite: 180]

Key Contrast: The author's Discourse Analysis is linguistic (about language-in-use). [cite_start][cite: 181] Foucault's Discourse is sociological (about systems of power and knowledge). [cite_start][cite: 182]

Semantics (System Meaning): The basic, literal, abstract, or "dictionary" meaning of a word or sentence, as defined by the rules of grammar, independent of any context. [cite_start][cite: 184]

  • Example: The system meaning of "Alman is dead" is simply that a person named Alman is "now not alive". [cite_start][cite: 185]

Situational Meaning: The specific, adapted, and nuanced meaning that arises when language is used in an actual context. [cite_start][cite: 186]

  • This is the primary focus of Discourse Analysis. [cite_start][cite: 187]
  • Example: The situational meaning of "Alman is dead" could be that his video game character died, or that he is spiritually "dead to his old self". [cite_start][cite: 188]

Key Connection: Discourse analysis studies how speakers and listeners use context to move from the abstract System Meaning (semantics) to a specific Situational Meaning. [cite_start][cite: 189]

This concept states that grammar provides a set of patterns that frame or "spin" reality in different ways. [cite_start][cite: 191]

Locator Pattern: A grammatical structure that locates a person or thing in relation to something else (a place, a group, or an abstract scale). [cite_start][cite: 192]

  • It describes a relational property. [cite_start][cite: 193]
  • Example: "John is in the 90th percentile" or "Johnnie is a '3' on the state test". [cite_start][cite: 194]
  • These are not inherent facts about John, but his position relative to a test. [cite_start][cite: 195]

Personal Property Pattern: A grammatical structure that attributes an inherent, internal quality or trait to a person. [cite_start][cite: 196]

  • Example: "John is cautious" or "John is honorable". [cite_start][cite: 197]

Category Error: The crucial mistake of treating a Locator Pattern statement as if it were a Personal Property Pattern statement. [cite_start][cite: 198]

  • Example: The teachers in the transcript slide from "Johnnie is a '3'" (Locator) to discussing the inherent properties of a "3" (e.g., being "self-initiated"). [cite_start][cite: 199]
  • They are turning an external test score into an internal personality trait. [cite_start][cite: 200]

Transfer Pattern: A grammatical structure that frames an event as the transfer of a thing (physical or abstract) from one person to another. [cite_start][cite: 201]

  • Example: "Mary taught biology to the students". This frames teaching as a transfer of content (biology) from teacher to student. [cite_start][cite: 202]
  • Contrast: This perspective differs from a Socratic view (drawing knowledge out of a student) or a skill-based view ("Mary taught the students how to do biology"). [cite_start][cite: 203]

Recipient Design (Speaker Task): How a speaker tailors their communication (style, tone, word choice) to fit their specific audience's perceived knowledge, status, or expectations. [cite_start][cite: 205]

[cite_start]

Positioning Design (Speaker Task): How a speaker uses language to frame a specific relationship by: [cite: 206]

  • Positioning themselves (e.g., as an "authority" or "friend"). [cite_start][cite: 207]
  • Attempting to get the listener to accept the corresponding identity (e.g., as a "subordinate" or "person needing advice"). [cite_start][cite: 208]

Situational Meaning Creation (Listener Task): The listener's active process of interpreting the speaker's language by using context to understand the intended situational meaning. [cite_start][cite: 209]

Response Design (Listener Task): The active, internal process where a listener prepares a response, which includes deciding whether to accept or reject the speaker's Positioning Design. [cite_start][cite: 210]

Social Languages: Specific adaptations, styles, or varieties of a language used by a social group to enact a specific identity. [cite_start][cite: 212]

  • Non-vernacular (Specialist): The language of specialist groups (e.g., the "way with words" of lawyers, doctors, gamers). [cite_start][cite: 213]
  • Vernacular: The "everyday" language used to enact an "everyday person" identity, learned from family and community. [cite_start][cite: 214]

Bid (for identity): An attempt by a speaker to get a specific socially significant identity recognized by others. [cite_start][cite: 215]

  • A bid can succeed, fail, or be negotiated. [cite_start][cite: 216]

"discourse" (little d): This is only the language part of an identity performance. [cite_start][cite: 217]

  • It is "interactive identity-based communication using language" (i.e., language-in-use). [cite_start][cite: 218]
  • Example: The actual words spoken by the gamers ("I died for you," "I got the heart"). [cite_start][cite: 219]

"Discourse" (Big D): This is the full performance of a socially significant identity. [cite_start][cite: 220]

  • It is the combination of "little d" discourse (language) PLUS all the non-language "stuff" like clothes, gestures, actions, props, values, and beliefs. [cite_start][cite: 221]
[cite_start]

Key Contrast & Formula: [cite: 222]

    [cite_start]
  • "little d" = Language (the "talk") [cite: 223]
    • [cite_start]
  • "Big D" = Language + Non-Language "Stuff" (the "walk") [cite: 224]
[cite_start]

Example: To perform the Big D Discourse of a "professor," you must use the right "little d" discourse" (an academic social language) AND wear the right clothes, be in the right place (a university), and act the right way. A professor lecturing drunk in a thong fails the Big D performance, even if their words ("little d") are correct. [cite: 225]

"Things" as Constructs: The chapter's foundational argument is that the "things" we perceive in the world (like a tree or a flower) are not objective, independent realities. [cite_start][cite: 3]

    [cite_start]
  • They are constructs based on our unique human sensory organs (a bee or a bat perceives a flower completely differently), cognitive processes, and the categories our language and culture provide [cite: 4].

Building with Language: The central theme is that humans use language to do more than just describe the world; [cite_start][cite: 5] we use it to actively build and shape our social realities. [cite_start][cite: 6]

Direct Speech Acts: These are the simplest examples of "building with language." [cite_start][cite: 7]

  • When said in the right circumstances, the words themselves create the reality. [cite_start][cite: 8]
  • Example: An umpire saying "Strike!" makes it a strike. [cite_start][cite: 9]
  • A minister saying "I now pronounce you..." makes the marriage happen. [cite_start][cite: 10]
    • [cite_start]
  • Saying "I promise" creates the promise [cite: 11].

When we speak or write, we are often simultaneously performing seven tasks. [cite_start][cite: 13] These tasks are the "social work" our language is doing. [cite_start][cite: 14] Each task provides a Discourse Analysis Question to use as a tool. [cite_start][cite: 15]

1. [cite_start]Significance (Building Significance) [cite: 16]

  • What it is: Using language to make certain things seem significant, important, or meaningful (or the opposite: trivial, normal, or insignificant). [cite_start][cite: 17]
  • Example: Saying "Hornworms sure vary a lot" uses a personal marker of attitude ("sure") to build significance. [cite_start][cite: 18] Saying "Hornworm growth exhibits a significant amount of variation" uses a technical term ("significant") to build a different, more "scientific" kind of significance. [cite_start][cite: 19]
DA Question: How is this piece of language being used to make certain things significant or not, and in what ways? [cite_start][cite: 20]

2. [cite_start]Practices (Building Activities) [cite: 21]

  • What it is: Using language to get others to recognize that a specific, socially-recognized "practice" or "activity" is happening. [cite_start][cite: 22]
  • Example: Speaking in one way to be "chit-chatting before a meeting" versus speaking in another way to be "formally opening the committee meeting." [cite_start][cite: 23]
  • Key Connection: This connects to the "chicken and egg" problem: the practice (like a meeting) gives our words meaning, but our words are what enact the practice and make it exist. [cite_start][cite: 24] They "bootstrap" each other. [cite_start][cite: 25]
DA Question: What practice (activity) is this piece of language being used to enact? [cite_start][cite: 26]

3. [cite_start]Identities (Building Identities) [cite: 27]

  • What it is: Using language to enact or "build" a specific identity or role for oneself in the current moment. [cite_start][cite: 28]
  • Key Tactic: We often build our own identity by simultaneously building or attributing an identity to others, often in comparison or contrast. [cite_start][cite: 29]
  • Example: Speaking as the "chair" of the committee (formal identity) versus speaking as a "peer/colleague" (informal identity). [cite_start][cite: 30]
DA Question: What identity is this piece of language being used to enact (for the speaker and for others)? [cite_start][cite: 31]

4. [cite_start]Relationships (Building Relationships) [cite: 32]

  • What it is: Using language to signal, create, and build a specific social relationship between the speaker and others (present or not). [cite_start][cite: 33]
  • Example: "Prof. Smith, I'm very sorry to have to move us on..." (builds a formal, deferential relationship). [cite_start][cite: 34] vs. "Ed, it's time to move on" (builds an informal, less deferential relationship with the same person). [cite_start][cite: 35]
    • [cite_start]
  • Key Connection: This task is directly related to Recipient Design from Chapter 2. [cite: 36]
DA Question: What sort of relationship is this piece of language seeking to enact? [cite_start][cite: 37]

5. [cite_start]Politics (Building "Social Goods") [cite: 38]

  • What it is: Using language to build a perspective on the distribution of social goods. [cite_start][cite: 39]
  • A "social good" is anything a society values, such as status, reputation, blame, guilt, or being seen as "normal," "good," or "innovative." [cite_start][cite: 40]
  • Example: How you talk about bugs in software. [cite_start][cite: 41]
    • "Microsoft loaded its OS with bugs" (Assigns blame; denies the social good of responsibility). [cite_start][cite: 42]
    • "Microsoft's OS is loaded with bugs" (Passive voice; mitigates blame). [cite_start][cite: 43]
    • "Like all innovative software, it has bugs" (Grants the social good of "being innovative" and reframes the bugs as a sign of that good). [cite_start][cite: 44]
DA Question: What perspective on social goods (what's "normal," "good," "high status," "blame-worthy," etc.) is this communicating? [cite_start][cite: 45]

6. [cite_start]Connections (Building Connections) [cite: 46]

  • What it is: Using language to connect or disconnect things; to make one thing relevant or irrelevant to another. [cite_start][cite: 47]
  • Example: Using the single term "fundamentalism" to describe both Christian and Islamic groups builds a connection between them, implying they are the same type of phenomenon (whether they are or not). [cite_start][cite: 48]
DA Question: How does this piece of language connect or disconnect things, or make one thing relevant to another? [cite_start][cite: 49]

7. [cite_start]Sign Systems & Knowledge (Building Privilege) [cite: 50]

  • What it is: Using language to privilege (treat as more valuable) or deprivilege (treat as less valuable) specific sign systems (e.g., technical language vs. everyday language; words vs. images) or ways of knowing (e.g., science vs. common sense; professor's knowledge vs. teacher's knowledge). [cite_start][cite: 51]
  • Example: In a meeting, acting in a way that makes the "knowledge and language of lawyers" the only valid way to discuss an issue, thus deprivileging "common sense" or other forms of expertise. [cite_start][cite: 52]
DA Question: How does this language privilege or deprivilege specific sign systems or ways of knowing? [cite_start][cite: 53]

This example shows how all seven tasks work together in a real conversation. [cite_start][cite: 55]

Context: A tense "town-gown" (university vs. local) relationship. A university professor (Sara) gets access to a public school teacher's (Karen) class by going through an administrator, which is a breach of protocol and seen as a power move. [cite_start][cite: 56]

The Key Quote (Karen): "I DID call her. And we BOTH expected to be around for the Summer Institute... I DID participate in it, but SARA wasn't able to do THAT." [cite_start][cite: 57]

[cite_start]

The 7-Task Analysis: [cite: 58]

  1. Significance: Karen uses stress ("DID," "BOTH," "SARA") and a "but..." contrast to make Sara's absence highly significant and not just a trivial fact. [cite_start][cite: 59]
  2. Practices: She is officially "giving a history" of the project, but she is using this practice to do social work (i.e., establish a hierarchy). [cite_start][cite: 60]
  3. Identities: She builds her own identity as a responsible, proactive "do-er" while building Sara's identity as a "not-doer" who is less committed. [cite_start][cite: 61]
  4. Relationships: She builds a "distanced but not particularly deferential" relationship with Sara (who is sitting right there). [cite_start][cite: 62]
  5. Politics: She denies Sara the "social good" of being a responsible partner, which is part of the larger political struggle over status between the university and the teachers. [cite_start][cite: 63]
  6. Connections: She explicitly connects her action ("I DID participate") to Sara's inaction ("but SARA wasn't") to create a damning contrast. [cite_start][cite: 64]
  7. Sign Systems & Knowledge: This exchange is the start of a long struggle over whose knowledge (professor's "research" vs. teacher's "classroom" knowledge) will be privileged in the project. [cite_start][cite: 65]

These are important "meta" ideas from the chapter about how to do discourse analysis. [cite_start][cite: 67]

Language as "Old and New": Language is like music. [cite_start][cite: 68]

  • Every time we speak, what we say is "old" because it follows the shared conventions and grammar of our social languages. [cite_start][cite: 69]
    • [cite_start]
  • At the same time, it is "new" because it is a unique performance by an individual, expressing their specific meaning and style [cite: 70].

Analysis is Hypothesis-Driven: Discourse analysis is an empirical investigation, not math. [cite_start][cite: 71]

  • The goal is to formulate hypotheses, not "definitive proof." [cite_start][cite: 72]
    • [cite_start]
  • You must always be open to disconfirming evidence [cite: 73].

Context-to-Language-to-Context: Analysis is a "two-way process." [cite_start]You use information about the context to form hypotheses about the language, and then you use a close study of the language to learn more about the context and how the speakers were interpreting it [cite: 74].

[cite_start]

The "Alternative Formulations" Test: A key method to figure out what a piece of language is doing is to ask: "In what other ways could this have been said?" [cite: 75]

    [cite_start]
  • By comparing the actual words to the alternatives, you can see the specific social work being done [cite: 76].
[cite_start]

Check for Reactions: You must check the data for how others react to the language (e.g., Prof. Vogel's attempted interruption), as this provides evidence for your hypothesis about what was being "built" [cite: 77].

This chapter introduces four new "Tools of Inquiry". [cite_start][cite: 80] These tools are used to analyze how the Seven Building Tasks (from Chapter 3) are accomplished in specific instances of language in use. [cite_start][cite: 81]

This tool focuses on how we use different varieties of language to signal our identity. [cite_start][cite: 83][cite_start] To understand this, the author first defines three key terms: [cite: 84]

  • Dialect: A variety of language associated with a specific region or social/ethnic group. [cite_start][cite: 85]
    • Examples: Southern English (regional), African American Vernacular English (ethnic). [cite_start][cite: 86]
  • Standard English: This is also a dialect, but it's the one associated with power, education, and media; [cite_start][cite: 87] it is not linguistically superior to other dialects. [cite_start][cite: 88]
  • Register: A variety of language used for a particular purpose, situation, or level of formality. [cite_start][cite: 89]
    • Examples: Formal legal language, academic writing, or the informal, "special purpose" language of gamers or biologists. [cite_start][cite: 90]
[cite_start]

🔑 Social Language (The Author's Umbrella Term): [cite: 91]

  • Definition: Any distinctive use of language—whether a dialect or a register—that is used to enact and recognize a socially distinctive identity. [cite_start][cite: 92]
  • It's a term that "cuts across all forms of language variation". [cite_start][cite: 93]
The Tool (as a question): "How do people use different varieties of language to enact and recognize different identities in different settings?". [cite_start][cite: 94]

Example: A student saying "Hornworms sure vary a lot in how big they get" (vernacular social language) vs. writing "Hornworm growth exhibits a significant amount of variation" (technical social language). [cite_start][cite: 95]

This tool expands on the "Social Language" tool by including non-language elements. [cite_start][cite: 97]

[cite_start]

Key Contrast: [cite: 98]

  • "little d" discourse: Refers only to language in use (e.g., conversations, stories). [cite_start][cite: 99]
  • "Big D" Discourse: Refers to a complete "identity kit" or "form of life". [cite_start][cite: 100]
    • It's the full performance of an identity, integrating words, acts, values, beliefs, attitudes, gestures, body positions, and clothes. [cite_start][cite: 101]

The Core Idea: You cannot just "talk the talk," you must also "walk the walk". [cite_start][cite: 102] Being a "scientist" or "skateboarder" involves a specific way of talking, acting, dressing, and valuing. [cite_start][cite: 103]

The Tool (as a question): "How do people use language and non-verbal things to enact and recognize different socially significant and historically created Discourses?". [cite_start][cite: 104]

Definition: The relationship between texts, showing how any given text (speech, writing, or media) refers to, quotes, alludes to, or is influenced by other texts. [cite_start][cite: 106]

  • "Text" (Broad Definition): Any structured arrangement of symbols that conveys meaning, including written documents, spoken language, images, audio, and video. [cite_start][cite: 107]
The Tool (as a question): "How do speakers and writers (and media designers) allude to, quote, or otherwise relate to other texts?". [cite_start][cite: 108]

Example: A Wired magazine article using the headline, "You got a problem with that?" [cite_start][cite: 109] This is an intertextual reference to "tough-guy" talk from movies. [cite_start][cite: 110]

Definition: Refers to the overarching, society-wide dialogues about major themes, debates, or issues that persist over time. [cite_start][cite: 112]

  • Examples: The "Conversations" about justice, democracy, climate change, or abortion. [cite_start][cite: 113]

Key Connection: This is "a type of intertextuality". When you speak, you are often alluding to one of these massive, ongoing Conversations. [cite_start][cite: 114]

Example: We understand "Smoking is associated with health problems" to mean smoking causes problems (and not the reverse) because we are all familiar with the long-running public "Conversation" about the dangers of smoking. [cite_start][cite: 115]

The Tool (as a question): "How do speakers, writers, and media designers allude to or relate to themes, debates, or motifs that have been the focus of much talk... in... society...?". [cite_start][cite: 116]

This is the most critical concept in the chapter. [cite_start][cite: 118]

The "who" and the "what": An utterance is only meaningful when it communicates a "who" (a socially-situated identity) and a "what" (a socially-situated practice/activity). [cite_start][cite: 119] It's best to see this as an integrated "who-doing-what". [cite_start][cite: 120]

🔑 The "Recognition" Principle: The key to Discourses is "recognition". [cite_start][cite: 121]

  • If you put language, action, values, and props together in a way that others recognize as a specific identity (e.g., "competent lawyer," "gamer"), you have successfully "pulled off" that Discourse. [cite_start][cite: 122]

Identity Crisis (Key Example): The Athabaskan student illustrates a conflict between Discourses. [cite_start][cite: 123]

  • The school-based Discourse of "writing an essay" requires a "major self-display" and "decontextualized" language, which directly conflicts with the Athabaskan Discourse, where silence is preferred when the audience relationship is unknown. [cite_start][cite: 124]

Analogy: Discourses as "Kits": Think of the Barbie-doll Discourse as a "kit". [cite_start][cite: 125]

  • This kit includes the doll's body shape, clothes, accessories, values, and ways of talking. [cite_start][cite: 126]
  • Society provides "innumerable kits" (scientist, gamer, feminist, etc.) that we use to live out our social lives. [cite_start][cite: 127]

Discourses are Not Fixed Units: They have no clear boundaries. [cite_start][cite: 128]

  • They can split (e.g., "natural philosophy" split into philosophy and physics). [cite_start][cite: 129]
  • They can meld or hybridize (e.g., mixed-ethnic gangs creating a "borderland Discourse"). [cite_start][cite: 130]
  • They emerge and die (e.g., the anti-racist skinhead Discourse died because it was confused with the racist one). [cite_start][cite: 131]
  • They are defined in relation to other Discourses (e.g., the "new male" Discourse emerged in response to feminism). [cite_start][cite: 132]
  • They don't have to be "grand" (e.g., the two different Discourses at opposite ends of a single bar). [cite_start][cite: 133]

1. [cite_start]The Core Distinction (Definition & Contrast) [cite: 135]

[cite_start]

"Discuss the author's concept of 'Big D' Discourse'. How is it different from 'little d' discourse' and a 'Social Language'? Finally, explain what the author means by describing a Discourse as an 'identity kit'." [cite: 136]

  • A "Big D" Discourse is the complete "identity kit" for being a certain "kind of person," like a scientist, gamer, or gang member. [cite_start][cite: 137]
    • [cite_start]
  • It's different from the other terms in this way: [cite: 138]
    • "little d" discourse: This is only language-in-use (e.g., a conversation or a story). [cite_start][cite: 139]
    • Social Language: This is a component of a Discourse. [cite_start][cite: 140] It's the specific style of language (a dialect or register) that a person uses to enact an identity. [cite_start][cite: 141]
    • "Big D" Discourse: This is the total package. [cite_start][cite: 142] It's the Social Language (the "talk") PLUS all the non-language "stuff": how you act, dress, think, value, and use props (the "walk"). [cite_start][cite: 143]
  • An "identity kit" is the author's analogy for all the required parts of a Discourse. [cite_start][cite: 144] Like a Barbie-doll "kit" contains the doll, the clothes, the accessories, and the values to "be Barbie", a "scientist kit" contains the lab coat, the technical language, the values (e.g., objectivity), and the tools needed to "be a scientist." [cite_start][cite: 145]

2. [cite_start]The Key Mechanism (Recognition) [cite: 146]

[cite_start]

"Discuss the principle of 'recognition' as the central mechanism for a 'Big D' Discourse. What does it mean to successfully 'pull off' a Discourse, and what happens when this 'recognition work' fails?" [cite: 147]

  • The "key to Discourses is 'recognition'". It's the central mechanism. [cite_start][cite: 148]
  • "Pulling off" a Discourse: You successfully "pull off" a Discourse when you combine all the parts of your "identity kit" (language, actions, values, props, etc.) in such a way that other people recognize you as a specific "kind of person" (a who) doing a specific activity (a what). [cite_start][cite: 149]
  • When Recognition Fails: "If it is not recognizable, then you are not 'in' the Discourse". [cite_start][cite: 150] The Discourse itself can even die. The text gives the example of the anti-racist skinhead Discourse, which was "dying out" because people, including the police, failed to recognize its members and confused them with the separate racist neo-Nazi Discourse. [cite_start][cite: 151]

3. [cite_start]The Key Problem (Conflict & Identity Crisis) [cite: 152]

[cite_start]

"Using the Scollon and Scollon (1981) study of Athabaskan students, discuss how Discourses can conflict. Explain what the author means by an 'identity crisis' and how the Discourse of 'school-based essayist prose' is 'mutually exclusive' with the Athabaskan Discourse." [cite: 153]

  • The Athabaskan students faced an "identity crisis" because the "way of being" (Discourse) required by their school was "mutually exclusive" with their native "way of being". [cite_start][cite: 154]
    • [cite_start]
  • This conflict happened for two main reasons: [cite: 155]
    1. Self-Display: The school's "essayist prose" Discourse required the student to make a "major self-display". [cite_start][cite: 156] In the Athabaskan Discourse, this is only appropriate for a person in a dominant position, which the student is not. [cite_start][cite: 157]
    2. Audience: The school Discourse requires writing for a "decontextualized" or unknown audience. [cite_start][cite: 158] In the Athabaskan Discourse, when the relationship with the audience is unknown, the preferred response is "silence". [cite_start][cite: 159]
  • Therefore, to "pull off" the identity of a "good student," the Athabaskan child was forced to act in a way that was not a "good Athabaskan," causing a "crisis in identity". [cite_start][cite: 160]

4. [cite_start]The Dynamic Nature (Boundaries & Change) [cite: 161]

[cite_start]

"The author argues that Discourses are not fixed 'units' and have 'no discrete boundaries'. Discuss this idea. How do Discourses change, and what is their relationship to other Discourses? Use examples from the text, such as Discourses splitting, melding, or dying." [cite: 162]

  • Discourses are not static "units" because they are historical, and people are always contesting and changing them; [cite_start][cite: 163] their "boundaries are always contestable". [cite_start][cite: 164]
  • Discourses are dynamic and defined in relation to each other. [cite_start]They can: [cite: 165]
    • Split: A single Discourse can divide. For example, medieval "natural philosophy" split into philosophy, physics, and other sciences. [cite_start][cite: 166]
    • Meld (Hybridize): Two or more Discourses can combine. For example, different ethnic gangs "melded" to form new "borderland Discourses". [cite_start][cite: 167]
    • Die: A Discourse can "die" when it's no longer performed or recognized. [cite_start][cite: 168] The anti-racist skinhead Discourse died because it was confused with the racist one. [cite_start][cite: 169]
    • Emerge in Response: New Discourses emerge in reaction to others. [cite_start][cite: 170] The "new male" Discourse emerged in the 1970s in response to feminism, and in turn, it changed the feminist Discourse. [cite_start][cite: 171]

This section uses one person (a young African American woman) telling the same story (about an earache) to two different audiences to show how social languages enact identities. [cite_start][cite: 228]

Text 1 (To Friends): Uses African American Vernacular English (AAVE). [cite_start][cite: 229]

  • Features: Repetition ("buggin' me"), vivid sound effects ("oooh oooh oooh oooh oooh"), and an informal, conversational style. [cite_start][cite: 230]
  • Function: An embodied performance that focuses on expressing emotion and personal, shared experience. [cite_start][cite: 231]
  • She is being a "peer". [cite_start][cite: 232]

Text 2 (To Researcher): Does not use AAVE. [cite_start][cite: 233]

  • Features: A clear setup ("after Edna an' me..."), specific informational details ("cotton... and some peroxide"), and a more formal (though still colloquial) narrative structure. [cite_start][cite: 234]
  • Function: An impersonal report that provides information and backgrounds emotion. [cite_start][cite: 235]
  • She is being a "student being interviewed". [cite_start][cite: 236]
[cite_start]

Key Implications of this Example: [cite: 237]

  • Co-construction: The speaker's identity is "co-constructed" by her and her audience. [cite_start][cite: 238] The interviewer isn't interviewing the "same person" who talks to her peers; [cite_start][cite: 239] he is getting a persona created in response to him. [cite_start][cite: 240] This raises questions about what interview data really means. [cite_start][cite: 241]
  • The "Real Self": The example asks: Who is the "real" person?. [cite_start][cite: 242] The Western view is that the "private" self (with friends) is "real". [cite_start][cite: 243] Other views hold that the "public" self (with the interviewer) is the "real" or "better" self. [cite_start][cite: 244]
  • Embodiment & AI: The girl's talk with her friends is "profoundly embodied". [cite_start][cite: 245] This is contrasted with Generative AI, which can mimic identity co-construction but lacks a body, feelings, and emotions, which are central to human cognition and caring. [cite_start][cite: 246]

Example 1: "Jane" (The "Hypocritical" Student) [cite: 248]

  • Context: A student, Jane, claimed she "did not use different social languages" because it would be "hypocritical". [cite_start][cite: 249] She recorded herself to prove it but proved the opposite. [cite_start][cite: 250]
  • Talk to Parents (About a story): A formal, "school-like" social language. [cite_start][cite: 251]
    • Features: Hedges ("I don't know," "it seemed to me"), formal vocabulary ("offensive," "callous," "hypocritical"), and complex sentences. [cite_start][cite: 252]
    • Identity Enacted: "A dutiful and intelligent daughter". [cite_start][cite: 253]
  • Talk to Boyfriend (Same story): An informal, "solidarity-building" social language. [cite_start][cite: 254]
    • Features: Direct claims, informal vocabulary ("ass," "guy"), direct address ("you know"), and leaving points to be inferred. [cite_start][cite: 255]
    • Identity Enacted: "A girlfriend being intimate with her boyfriend". [cite_start][cite: 256]

Social languages are marked by two aspects of grammar: [cite: 269]

  1. Aspect 1 (Traditional): The specific units used (e.g., nouns, verbs, clauses). [cite_start][cite: 270]
  2. Aspect 2 (Collocational): The patterns created when grammatical units "co-locate" (or cluster) together. [cite_start][cite: 271]
  • These patterns are what signal or "index" a characteristic "who-doing-what". [cite_start][cite: 272]
  • Analogy: Choosing clothes. A single item (like flip-flops) doesn't mean much. [cite_start][cite: 273] But when flip-flops "co-locate" with a bathing suit, sunglasses, and a sun hat, the pattern signals a clear identity and activity (beach-goer). [cite_start][cite: 274]
  • Linguistic Example: In Jane's talk to her boyfriend, informal words ("ass," "guy") co-locate with informal devices ("you know") to form a pattern that clearly signals an informal, solidarity-building social language. [cite_start][cite: 275]

This final section argues that "formal" academic language is NOT "decontextualized" or more explicit than informal language. [cite_start][cite: 277] It is just as context-dependent. [cite_start][cite: 278]

The Sentence: "Lung cancer death rates are clearly associated with an increase in smoking". [cite_start][cite: 279]

The "Trash Compactor" Problem: This sentence is full of nominalizations (turning whole sentences into noun phrases), like "lung cancer death rates" and "an increase in smoking". [cite_start][cite: 280]

The Ambiguity: These nominalizations are "compacted trash"; you can't be sure what information went into them. [cite_start][cite: 281]

  • "lung cancer death rates" could mean "how many people die" OR "how fast people die" OR "how many lungs die," etc. (4+ meanings). [cite_start][cite: 282]
  • "are associated with" could mean "causes," "is caused by," or just "is correlated with" (4+ meanings). [cite_start][cite: 283]
  • "an increase in smoking" could mean "people smoke more" OR "more people smoke," etc. (7+ meanings). [cite_start][cite: 284]

The Result: The grammar alone produces 112+ possible meanings (4×4×7). [cite_start][cite: 285]

The Mystery: Why does everyone instantly and unconsciously pick the same single meaning (that smoking causes more people to die from lung cancer)?. [cite_start][cite: 286]

The Answer: The "Big C" Conversation. We all pick the same meaning not because of the grammar, but because we are "privy to the ongoing discussion in our society about smoking". [cite_start][cite: 287]

  • The Conversation is more important than the grammar for fixing the meaning. [cite_start][cite: 288]

The Conclusion: "Formal" language is not decontextualized. [cite_start][cite: 289]

  • It only makes sense when you use its specific Conversational context. [cite_start][cite: 290]
  • To teach this language is to embed students "in the Conversational sea in which [it] swims". [cite_start][cite: 291]
